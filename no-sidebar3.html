Ultraviolet radiation (UV) is a type of electromagnetic radiation that has a higher energy and shorter wavelength than visible light. UV can damage the DNA and proteins of living cells, causing mutations, cancers, and other diseases. However, UV can also stimulate the production of vitamin D in animals, which is essential for bone health and immune system function. Moreover, UV can split water molecules in the upper atmosphere, creating oxygen atoms that can combine with other oxygen molecules to form ozone. An ozone layer in the atmosphere is essential for life, as it absorbs most of the harmful UV rays, minimizing the exposure of organisms. Knowing this, since the star is cooler than the sun, its maximum emission shifts to red, so it will radiate less in the UV spectrum. This raises the possibility that organisms will develop less skin cancer and diseases from UV light, but will still be able to produce sufficient vitamin D amounts. Additionally, we should consider the effect of the star's color on the sky and the vision of life forms. The color of a star depends on its surface temperature, which determines the peak wavelength of its emitted light. K-type stars are cooler than the Sun, so they emit more light in the infrared spectrum than in the visible spectrum. This means that their light appears redder to human eyes than the Sun's light. The color of the sky also depends on the scattering of light by the atmosphere. Since blue light is scattered more than red light, the sky appears blue on Earth. However, on a planet around a K-type star, the sky would appear dimmer and redder, since there would be less blue light to scatter. The vision and color perception of life forms on such a planet could adapt to the redder environment, or they could develop other ways to sense their surroundings, such as infrared or ultrasonic vision.
